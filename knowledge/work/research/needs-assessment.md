An IRB-approved three-phase needs assessment study to address research silos in the OHSU Emergency Medicine department. ### Research Leadership Focus Group - Detailed Thematic Findings

**Focus Group Details:**
- Date: January 12, 2026
- Duration: ~60 minutes  
- Participants: 9 research leaders (Brandon Maughan, Matt Hansen, Bory Kea, David Sheridan, Joshua Lupton, Jenny Cook, Denise, Nathan Bahr, Abby Dotson)
- Format: Virtual, audio recorded and transcribed

**Eight Major Themes Identified (Organized into Structural and Human/Relational Factors):**

**STRUCTURAL BARRIERS:**

**Theme 1: The Craig Newgard Transition** - Loss of central coordinating figure revealed coordination infrastructure gaps. Craig served multiple critical functions: knowledge broker connecting people with opportunities, matchmaker pairing complementary skills, gatekeeper managing participation appropriately, and resource coordinator. His departure (with significant P&T committee influence) created natural inflection point for systematic change as department adapts to new leadership under Matt Hansen.

**Theme 2: Generator vs. Finder Identity** - Research leaders see themselves as opportunity "generators" rather than "seekers," creating asymmetric information flow. Leaders generate opportunities (grants, projects, collaborations) but lack effective mechanisms for broadcasting these to potential collaborators. This identity mismatch means those who have opportunities aren't actively seeking collaborators through existing channels.

**Theme 3: Visibility Crisis** - Even research leaders lack comprehensive knowledge of ongoing projects within the department. Jenny Cook (19 years institutional memory) noted this problem emerged with departmental growth over 10-15 years - organic networking that worked at small scale insufficient at current scale. Leaders don't know what research is happening, who is doing what, or where expertise resides even within their own department.

**Theme 4: Departmental Growth & Evolution** - Department transitioned from small focused team where informal connections worked to large diverse portfolio requiring systematic infrastructure. What functioned through hallway conversations and personal relationships at 30 people fails at 100+ people. Scale necessitates different coordination mechanisms.

**HUMAN/RELATIONAL FACTORS:**

**Theme 5: The Engagement Paradox** - **CRITICAL FINDING**: Successful collaboration requires initiative, yet potential collaborators don't know where to start or what to do. Evidence of paradox severity:
  - Zero residents enrolled in research residency track for 2 consecutive years
  - Zero residents attended research career session  
  - Residents explicitly stated "NO research" in career planning
  - Initiative and knowledge create catch-22: need initiative to engage but need knowledge to take initiative
  - "We can't go find them if they aren't coming" (Jenny Cook)

**Theme 6: Specificity Problem** - Research leaders need specific information from potential collaborators (interests, time commitment available, skills, experience level) to make good matches. However, junior faculty and trainees often lack clarity themselves about what they want, what they can offer, or how much time they can commit, making matching difficult.

**Theme 7: Expectation Management** - Mismatched expectations about research commitment and follow-through creates frustration. Leaders expect sustained engagement and follow-through when people join projects, but don't always receive it. Lack of clear expectation-setting upfront about time commitment, responsibilities, and deliverables contributes to problems.

**Theme 8: Communication & Dissemination Gap** - Current methods (email, departmental website, word of mouth) inadequate for effective opportunity broadcasting and discovery. Email gets lost or ignored, website static and not consulted regularly, word of mouth only reaches those already in networks. Need better mechanisms for push notifications and pull discovery.

**Cross-Cutting Insights:**
- Leadership transition creates natural inflection point with reduced resistance to change
- Multiple themes interconnect: visibility crisis + generator identity + communication gap = engagement paradox
- System-level solutions needed (not just individual behavior change)
- Tool requirements emerging: discoverability, ease of use, broad adoption critical

**UTAUT Framework Mapping:**
- **Performance Expectancy**: Tool must demonstrably increase visibility, reduce time to find collaborators, improve match quality
- **Effort Expectancy**: Must be visually appealing ("legible, not just rows and cells"), easy to scan, not overwhelming, intuitive without training
- **Social Influence**: MOST discussed construct - broad adoption necessity emphasized repeatedly ("has to be adopted broadly," "important that other people use it")
- **Facilitating Conditions**: Need institutional support, leadership endorsement, champion users, integration with existing workflows

### Qualitative Analysis Approach
**Multi-Layered Analytical Strategy:**
1. **Initial Inductive Coding** (Pure CGT): Let themes emerge organically from data without imposing predetermined frameworks, respecting constructivist grounded theory principles
2. **Deductive Mapping to UTAUT Framework**: After emergent coding, map themes to UTAUT constructs (Performance Expectancy, Effort Expectancy, Social Influence, Facilitating Conditions) to understand technology acceptance factors
3. **Cross-Cutting Analysis**: Examine patterns across different question types (opening â†’ transition â†’ key â†’ UTAUT â†’ closing) to identify tensions, contradictions, and areas of consensus
4. **Stakeholder-Specific Pattern Identification**: Recognize perspectives unique to each stakeholder group (leadership vs. junior faculty vs. trainees)
5. **Validation**: Check themes against raw data to ensure interpretations are grounded in participant language and meaning

**Analytical Process Documentation:**
Created comprehensive analytical audit trail documenting complete coding journey:
- Initial reading with first impressions and questions
- Open coding: Line-by-line analysis with 150+ initial codes and analytical memos written during coding
- Six detailed analytical memos exploring emerging patterns ("The Craig Question," "The Engagement Paradox," "Generators vs. Finders," etc.)
- Focused coding: Grouping 150+ initial codes into 8 preliminary categories
- Axial coding: Mapping relationships and connections between categories
- Theoretical integration: Specific decisions about theme construction and organization
- UTAUT mapping: Deductive analysis layer connecting emergent themes to technology acceptance constructs
- Quality assessment: Evaluation using Lincoln & Guba trustworthiness criteria (credibility through prolonged engagement and thick description, transferability through rich contextual description, dependability through transparent audit trail, confirmability through linking interpretations to evidence)
